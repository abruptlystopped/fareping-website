Cruise price dropped after you booked? Here's exactly what to do.

Prices on cruise sailings move all the time — sometimes dramatically, and sometimes just days after you booked. Most cruisers don't know they can often rebook at the lower rate, or they find out too late. Here's the complete playbook.

First: why cruise prices drop after you book

Cruise lines use dynamic pricing, the same way airlines and hotels do. A cabin that sold for $1,800 in January might be $1,200 in March if inventory isn't moving, or during a promotional sale period. The line's goal is to fill every cabin — so prices flex based on demand, season, and how far out the sailing is.

Common reasons you might see a price drop after booking:

  • A new promotional sale (BOGO deposits, free gratuities, onboard credit offers)
  • Inventory still unsold as the sail date approaches
  • Repositioning or itinerary changes the cruise line needs to compensate for
  • Seasonal pricing windows

The catch: a lower price doesn't automatically mean you get it. And once you're past your cancellation-penalty window, you're usually locked in at what you paid. Timing matters enormously.

Can you actually get the lower price?

Almost always — but only if you're still within your refundable window. The standard process is:

  1. You cancel your existing booking (which refunds your deposit)
  2. You rebook the same cabin or equivalent at the new lower rate
  3. Any current promotions on the new booking may also apply

Some cruise lines will apply the lower rate directly to your existing reservation without a full cancel-and-rebook, but this varies by line and agent.

Critical: Once you cross your final payment date or enter the cancellation penalty window, you generally cannot rebook without losing a portion of your fare. Know your deadline — and watch for price drops before you hit it.

Step-by-step: how to rebook at the lower price

1. Verify the price drop is real and current

Go directly to the cruise line's website and price out your exact sailing, ship, cabin category, and number of guests. Don't rely on a screenshot from yesterday — prices can change hourly, and you need to confirm the rate is still available before you call. Note the exact price, the cabin category, and any promotions attached to the new rate.

2. Check your cancellation deadline

Find your booking confirmation email and locate the cancellation policy section. This is your hard deadline. If you're before the penalty window, you're in good shape. If your final payment date hasn't passed, even better.

3. Call — don't email

This is not a situation for a web form. Call the cruise line's customer service line directly. If you booked through a travel agent, call your agent instead (they own the booking and must make changes). Have ready:

  • Your confirmation number
  • The exact current price you're seeing on their website
  • The cabin category and sailing date
  • Your original price paid
"Hi, I have a booking on [sailing / ship] on [date], confirmation number [XXXXXX]. I noticed the current price for [cabin category] on that sailing is [new price], and I originally booked at [original price]. I'm still within my refundable window and I'd like to ask if I can have the current rate applied to my reservation, or if I need to cancel and rebook. Can you help me with that?"

4. Ask about current promotions

The new lower price might also come bundled with a promotion — onboard credit, free gratuities, a drink package. Ask what's currently attached to that rate. Sometimes a promotion has more value than the raw price difference.

5. Confirm everything in writing

Once the change is made, ask for a new confirmation email. Verify the new price, any promotions, and your updated cancellation policy. Save this email — it's your new booking record.

How much can you actually save?

It varies widely. On a Disney cruise, a price drop of $200–$500 per person isn't unusual, especially around promotional periods. On a Royal Caribbean sailing, we've seen guests save $300–$800 on a family cabin by rebooking during a flash sale.

The key insight: these savings are available to anyone who notices the drop and acts before their deadline. Most people don't notice. They book, stop checking, and sail at the original price — even when a lower rate was available for weeks.

The problem with doing this manually

To catch a price drop before your cancellation window closes, you'd need to check your cruise's pricing regularly — ideally every day or two — from the moment you book until your final payment date. That's weeks or months of checking. Miss a window and you've left real money on the table.

Frequently asked questions

What if I booked through a travel agent?

Your agent owns the booking, so all changes must go through them. Contact your agent when you spot a price drop — the process is the same, but they make the call on your behalf. A good agent will proactively monitor this for you, though many don't.

Does it matter what rate type I booked?

Yes. Some promotional or "Early Saver" rates include price protection guarantees. Other rates — particularly deeply discounted non-refundable fares — may not allow cancellation and rebooking at all. Check your booking terms carefully. If you're unsure, call and ask.

Can I get a price adjustment after my final payment date?

Generally no, at least not through the standard cancel-and-rebook method. After the final payment date, penalties apply to cancellations. Occasionally a cruise line will offer onboard credit as a goodwill gesture if you call and ask politely, but don't count on it. This is why monitoring before the deadline matters so much.

What if the lower price is for a different cabin category?

It may still be worth calling. If a higher category is now priced below what you paid for your current cabin, you might be able to upgrade for free or a small additional cost. Or you can rebook your original category at whatever the current rate is. It's always worth the phone call.

How do I know when to stop monitoring?

Your final payment date is the key milestone. After that, you can no longer rebook without penalty, so price monitoring stops being actionable. FarePing tracks your booking timeline automatically and lets you know when that window is closing.
