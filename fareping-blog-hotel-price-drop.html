Hotel tips · June 2026

Hotel price dropped after you booked? Here's how to get the lower rate.

Hotel rates fluctuate constantly — and the rate you paid the day you booked is almost never the lowest price available for your stay. Most guests never check again. The ones who do often save hundreds of dollars with a single phone call.

Editorial note: Most of the easy hotel savings come from refundable rates you can still change before check-in.

Why hotel prices drop after you book

Hotels use the same revenue management systems as airlines: prices shift based on occupancy, demand, and how far out your stay is. A room that costs $220 a night in February might be $160 in April — not because the hotel changed, but because they need to fill beds.

Common triggers for a rate drop:

  • Lower demand than expected as your check-in date approaches
  • A new promotional sale or member rate added after your booking
  • A third-party site (Expedia, Hotels.com) running a discount the hotel matches
  • A last-minute inventory push in the days before check-in

The window matters: You can almost always rebook at a lower rate while you're in the free cancellation window. Once you cross your cancellation deadline on a non-refundable rate, your options narrow significantly.

The major chains all have Best Rate Guarantee programs — but they typically only apply to rates found on competing third-party sites, not to rates that drop on the hotel's own site after you book. For a rate drop on the same site you booked through, your best move is to cancel and rebook (if refundable) or call and ask directly.

Step-by-step: how to get the lower hotel rate

1. Confirm the lower rate is still available

Go to the hotel's website and price out your exact room type, dates, and number of guests. Note the rate, any terms attached to it, and whether it's refundable or non-refundable. Prices can change in minutes — you need to confirm it's live before you call.

2. Check your cancellation terms

Open your booking confirmation and find the cancellation policy. If you're in the free cancellation window, you can cancel and rebook without any penalty. If you're past it, your options depend on the hotel's flexibility and your loyalty status.

3. Try the easiest path first: cancel and rebook

For a refundable booking, the cleanest move is often to cancel it online and immediately book the lower rate. This takes minutes and doesn't require a phone call. Make sure the lower rate is still showing before you cancel.

Do not cancel before the new rate is confirmed available. Prices can disappear while you're mid-process. Have the new booking page open in a separate tab before you cancel your existing reservation.

4. Call the hotel if you want them to adjust the rate directly

Some hotels will apply the lower rate to your existing reservation without making you cancel and rebook — especially for loyalty members. Call the property directly (not the chain's 800 number) and speak to the front desk or reservations manager.

"Hi, I have a reservation at your property under [name], confirmation number [XXXXXX], checking in on [date]. I noticed the current rate for [room type] on that date is now [lower price] on your website, and I originally booked at [original price]. I'm still within my free cancellation window — is there any way you can apply the lower rate to my existing reservation without me having to cancel and rebook?"

5. Ask about loyalty rate adjustments

If you're a loyalty member of Hilton Honors, Marriott Bonvoy, or World of Hyatt, mention it. Loyalty status often unlocks more flexibility on rate adjustments. Some programs have dedicated customer service lines where adjustments are handled faster.

6. Get confirmation in writing

Once any adjustment is made, ask for a new confirmation email with the updated rate. Save it and verify the check-in date, room type, rate, and cancellation policy all look correct.

What if I booked through a third-party site?

If you booked through Expedia, Hotels.com, Booking.com, or a similar OTA, the hotel often cannot modify your reservation directly — the OTA owns the booking. Your options are:

  1. Cancel through the OTA and rebook at the lower rate (if still in your cancellation window)
  2. Call the OTA's customer service and ask if they can apply the lower rate
  3. Check if the OTA has its own Price Match or Price Drop protection

Third-party bookings are generally less flexible for rate adjustments, which is one of the reasons booking directly with the hotel is usually the better move when price monitoring is a priority.

How much can you save?

Hotel rate drops vary widely. For a weekend leisure stay at a major chain, seeing a $30–$80 per night drop during a sale period is common. For longer stays or higher-end hotels, the gap can be $150 or more per night. Multiply that by three or four nights and it's a meaningful savings on a trip you've already planned.

Frequently asked questions

Does this work for Airbnb or vacation rentals?

Airbnb and VRBO rates are set by individual hosts and don't have the same dynamic pricing systems as hotel chains. Rate drops are less common and there's no equivalent cancel-and-rebook process. Your best option is to message the host directly if you see a lower price for the same period.

Can I get a price adjustment on a non-refundable hotel rate?

Generally no through the standard process, but it's always worth a polite phone call to the hotel. Front desk managers and property general managers sometimes have discretion to apply onboard credit, upgrades, or a rate adjustment as a goodwill gesture — especially for loyalty members, extended stays, or significant price drops. You have nothing to lose by asking.

What if the lower rate is for a different room type?

This happens often: a room category above yours gets repriced below what you paid for your current room. In this case, call the hotel and ask if you can upgrade to that room type at the current price, or if you can have your original room type adjusted to match the new market rate. Either way, it's worth the conversation.

How often do hotel prices drop?

Prices on a given property can change dozens of times before your check-in date. The most significant drops often happen 2–4 weeks before arrival when hotels push to fill remaining inventory, or during flash sales that the chain runs without notice. The challenge is that these windows close fast — monitoring manually means you'll miss most of them.
